Where We Are

How the Program Works

Every session fields four courts, ranked top to bottom, run as a liquid ladder. Rosters are built by position, not overall rank. A Week 1 baseline test seeds a positional depth chart (setters, outsides, and so on), and each court is assembled top-down so it's a playable team. You advance by beating the player above you at your position.

  • The 1-2 and 3-4 boundaries move weekly, earned off the standards board and Wednesday league results.
  • The 2-3 boundary moves at phase checkpoints (after weeks 2, 4, and 6), with a guaranteed minimum number of players moving up each time.
  • Swing players, the top few on court 3, train with courts 1-2 on Mondays and play their home court on Wednesdays.
  • Riser call-ups: each Wednesday, one or two standouts from courts 3-4 play league night up a court.
  • Placement is private (each player knows her own court and what she's chasing) but the pathway is public: posted standards, weekly rosters, and celebrated promotions.

Demo-disperse teaching: all four courts run the identical session card, scaled by level. New material is taught once, centrally, by the fall ball lead, with the whole gym watching (a 4-minute cap, twice a night), then courts disperse and run it: same drill names and cue words everywhere, just different numbers for entry difficulty, tempo, and reps.

One gym clock, one whistle: balls in carts, 90-second transitions, no water until the whistle says so.

The Fall League

Match play is tiered so it's competitive at every level: a performance league (4 squads, courts 1-2) and a development league (4 squads, courts 3-4), each running its own standings table.

Squads are drawn from the whole pool, not just one court, and built by a positional draft so every squad has a setter, a few pins, a middle, and a defender. Squads are redrafted at every phase checkpoint, so the league genuinely changes shape as the ladder moves.

League formats rotate by phase: standards races early, then wash games and tournaments, then Spanish Six / Arkansas / 5-4-3-2-1 brackets for the finale stretch.

The standards leaderboard is gym-wide and pool-blind: perfect-pass counts, tape flips, talk counts, and improvement points go on one board for all 60 players. A development-league player can lead the entire gym, and several will.

Week-by-Week Schedule

Managing Competitiveness at Scale

  1. Tier stigma and false hope. The ladder is framed positionally ("you're the #5 outside," not "a court 3 kid"), courts 1-2 and 3-4 churn weekly so placement feels like this week's lineup rather than an identity, and the gym-wide leaderboard is court-blind. The pathway is objective, visibly alive (guaranteed movement, weekly risers), and never oversold. Nobody promises a team spot.
  2. The best-coach allocation trap. The natural pull is to put the strongest coach on the top court. We resist it: the development league gets the highest-energy, best-teaching coach, because that's where retention and the club's future rosters live.
  3. Coach consistency. The consequences policy is written and covered at the coaches' meeting; the fall ball lead owns all gym-wide consequences so no court runs meaningfully harder or softer than another.
  4. Anonymity at 60 players. Each home-court coach owns their group of 15, knows names by week one, and writes progress notes; partner pairings rotate within courts every session.
  5. Gym-wide theater. All-gym tape-flip wars, a records board for controllables (best talk count, best pass average, most improved), and a finale night in front of 60 families.

Fall ball placement isn't team selection, but the week 1 and week 8 test numbers double as honest, objective backup for later tryout conversations.

Testing at Scale

Wednesday of Week 1 and Monday of Week 8 are test nights. The four courts become test stations and groups rotate through all of them:

  • Serving: 10 serves, in-count plus tape flips
  • Passing: 20 coach float serves, scored and averaged
  • OOS Setting: 10 balls from middle back to a pin target, hittable count
  • Approach touch, plus a 2-minute one-arm talk count

Every player gets a baseline in Week 1 and a retest in Week 8, so report cards can show real before-and-after numbers.

Staffing & Coach Prep

Staffing target: one lead coach plus one helper per court (8 people total), plus the fall ball lead (Hayley or Song) as floor director, unattached to any court, running the whistle and floating.

Coaches meet before Aug 10 to walk the training packet, cover the demo-disperse protocol, the consequences policy, and the whistle rules, and to rep their own entries. Each court coach gets the week's session card in advance with drill flow, level parameters, and the shared cue-word list, and echoes the fall ball lead's exact words so one demo can stretch across four courts. Court coaches are generalists running the full card, not station specialists; the fall ball lead does all central teaching. A 10-minute coach huddle follows every session.

Communication Plan

  • Kickoff email (early August): dates, groups and what placement means, the five standards, league explanation, finale date.
  • Weekly Thursday email: what the gym trained, one thing to ask your kid, and a look at next week.
  • Week 4: a one-line progress note per player from her home-court coach.
  • Finale: report cards handed to families in person: baseline vs. retest, two things owned, one thing to chase next.

Munciana Integration

The program blends two coaching systems: A5 rigidity on technique and cue words, Munciana flexibility on drill parameters: A5 teaches the touch, Munciana teaches the move and the scoreboard.

  • Phase 1: the Two-Man Routine in warm-ups, kneeling and single-leg serves ahead of the tape progression, Speedball as the closer.
  • Phase 2: the tilt family and Two-Ball Passing for rotational-passing volume, noodle and seam-box serve targets.
  • Phase 3: the Munciana defense curriculum (Three-Way Release, Seat to Seam, Go Stay), Five-and-Five as the gym-wide out-of-system standard, the Mia Drill alongside Reverse Fire.
  • Phase 4: weekly league engines (Biggie Smalls, Momentum, Net Six, Hand-to-Hand Combat, and more), with A5's Arkansas, Spanish Six, and 5-4-3-2-1 reserved for checkpoints and the finale.

Every drill runs both left and right, in 45-second increments, with serving before and after every water break.

Culture Standards

  1. On time means early; bags lined up; phones zipped in bags.
  2. Talking, running, and shouting at all times, on every court.
  3. No balls on the floor. Everyone shags, and everyone watches the adjacent courts.
  4. Do it correctly first, then chase the record.
  5. Coaches hold the same bar: entries are practiced skills, transitions are on the whistle, teaching language is uniform.

Contingencies

  • Coach shortage: collapse to three stations, or pair two groups on the compete court with a helper refereeing.
  • Shortened slot (90 min): the teach and system/league blocks get trimmed; the daily constants and demos stay intact.
  • Turnout swings: below 45 players, drop to six league squads and three league courts; the fourth becomes a skills court.
  • Labor Day: folded into the packed Wednesday, September 9th session.
