Where We Are

How the Program Works

Every session fields four courts, ranked top to bottom, run as a liquid ladder. Rosters are built by position, not overall rank. A Week 1 baseline test seeds a positional depth chart (setters, outsides, and so on), and each court is assembled top-down so it's a playable team. You advance by beating the player above you at your position.

  • The Day 1 baseline test seeds the positional depth chart and gives every player an initial placement onto courts 1-2 or courts 3-4.
  • Players move within courts 1-2 and within courts 3-4 weekly, based on performance.
  • The 2-3 boundary moves at phase checkpoints (end of weeks 2, 4, and 6), with a minimum of 3 players moving each time.
  • Swing players: top players on court 3 train with courts 1-2 on Mondays and play their home court on Wednesdays, which also helps cover absences.
  • Riser call-ups: each Wednesday, one or two standouts from courts 3-4 play league night up a court, again helping fill for absences.
  • Courts are posted ahead of time, before each session, so every player knows where she's playing.

Demo-disperse teaching: all four courts run the identical session card, scaled by level. New material is taught once, centrally, by Hayley (with Song helping on demos), with the whole gym watching (a 4-minute cap, twice on Mondays), then courts disperse and run it: same drill names and cues everywhere, with minor adjustments for entry difficulty, tempo, and reps.

The gym operates on one clock, one whistle: balls in carts, 90-second transitions, water breaks, and more.

The Fall League

Match play is tiered so it's competitive at every level: a performance league (4 squads, courts 1-2) and a development league (4 squads, courts 3-4), each running its own standings table.

Squads are drawn from the whole pool, not just one court, and built by a positional draft so every squad has a setter, a few pins, a middle, and a defender. Squads are redrafted at every phase checkpoint, so the league genuinely changes shape as the ladder moves.

No standards races. League formats ramp entry difficulty across the season: free ball entry and wash games early, then Hand-to-Hand Combat and Biggie Smalls, building to full six-on-six with live serve and serve-receive by Net Six, Spanish Six, Arkansas, and the 5-4-3-2-1 finale.

Week-by-Week Schedule

Managing Competitiveness at Scale

  1. Tier stigma and false hope. The ladder is framed positionally ("you're the #5 outside," not "a court 3 kid"), and courts 1-2 and 3-4 churn weekly so placement feels like this week's lineup rather than an identity. The pathway is objective, visibly alive (guaranteed movement, weekly risers), and never oversold. Nobody promises a team spot.
  2. The best-coach allocation trap. The natural pull is to put the strongest coach on the top court. We resist it: the development league gets the highest-energy, best-teaching coach, because that's where retention and the club's future rosters live.
  3. Coach consistency. The consequences policy is written and covered at the coaches' meeting; Hayley owns all gym-wide consequences so no court runs meaningfully harder or softer than another.
  4. Anonymity at 60 players. Each home-court coach owns their group of 15, knows names by week one, and writes progress notes; partner pairings rotate within courts every session.
  5. Gym-wide theater. All-gym tape-flip wars, and a finale night in front of 60 families.

Fall ball placement isn't team selection, but the week 1 and week 8 test numbers double as honest, objective backup for later tryout conversations.

Testing at Scale

Wednesday of Week 1 and Monday of Week 8 are test nights. The four courts become test stations and groups rotate through all of them:

  • Serving: 10 serves, in-count plus tape flips
  • Passing: 20 coach float serves, scored and averaged
  • OOS Setting: 10 balls from middle back to a pin target, hittable count
  • Approach touch, plus a 2-minute one-arm talk count

Every player gets a baseline in Week 1 and a retest in Week 8, so report cards can show real before-and-after numbers.

Staffing & Coach Prep

Staffing target: one lead coach plus one helper per court (8 people total), plus Hayley, the fall ball lead, as floor director (with Song helping on demo nights), unattached to any court, running the whistle and floating.

Coaches meet before Aug 10 to walk the training packet, cover the demo-disperse protocol, the consequences policy, and the whistle rules, and to rep their own entries. Each court coach gets the week's session card in advance with drill flow, level parameters, and the shared cue-word list, and echoes Hayley's exact words so one demo can stretch across four courts. Court coaches are generalists running the full card, not station specialists; Hayley does all central teaching, with Song helping on demo nights. A 10-minute coach huddle follows every session.

Communication Plan

  • Kickoff email (early August): dates, groups and what placement means, the five standards, league explanation, finale date.
  • Weekly Thursday email: what the gym trained, one thing to ask your kid, and a look at next week.
  • Week 4: a one-line progress note per player from her home-court coach.
  • Finale: report cards handed to families in person: baseline vs. retest, two things owned, one thing to chase next.

Munciana Integration

The program blends two coaching systems: A5 rigidity on technique and cue words, Munciana flexibility on drill parameters: A5 teaches the touch, Munciana teaches the move and the scoreboard.

  • Phase 1, Ball Control Engine: the Two-Man Routine in warm-ups, kneeling and single-leg serves ahead of the tape progression, the tilt family as rotational-passing vehicles, Speedball and Mountain D as closers.
  • Phase 2, Defense: the Munciana defense curriculum (Three-Way Release, Seat to Seam, Go Stay, Campfire D), Hand-to-Hand Combat as the league engine.
  • Phase 3, Attacking: Reverse Fire as the spine across both weeks, the Lamb progression for exchange-compete pressure, Biggie Smalls as the first six-on-six league engine.
  • Phase 4, Systems & Out of System: Directional Pass with the always-finish-in-cover habit, Five-and-Five as the gym-wide out-of-system standard, the Mia Drill, Net Six as the OOS league engine, and A5's Arkansas, Spanish Six, and 5-4-3-2-1 as finale events.

Every drill runs both left and right, in 45-second increments, with serving before and after every water break.

Culture Standards

  1. On time means early; bags lined up; phones zipped in bags.
  2. Talking, running, and shouting at all times, on every court.
  3. No balls on the floor. Everyone shags, and everyone watches the adjacent courts.
  4. Do it correctly first, then chase the record.
  5. Coaches hold the same bar: entries are practiced skills, transitions are on the whistle, teaching language is uniform.

Contingencies

  • Coach shortage: collapse to three stations, or pair two groups on the compete court with a helper refereeing.
  • Shortened slot (90 min): the teach and system/league blocks get trimmed; the daily constants and demos stay intact.
  • Turnout swings: below 45 players, drop to six league squads and three league courts; the fourth becomes a skills court.
  • Labor Day: folded into the packed Wednesday, September 9th session.
